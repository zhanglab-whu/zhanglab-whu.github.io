Research

Single-molecule technologies for nucleic-acid mechanics, molecular detection, and cell mechanobiology

The lab combines magnetic tweezers, single-molecule fluorescence, biochemical assays, and molecular dynamics simulations to connect molecular structure with biological function.

Core Themes

Research areas

DNA mechanical probes and molecular detection

Click to view how programmable DNA probes connect single-molecule mechanics with cell biology and clinical samples.

The lab develops DNA and RNA molecular probes for single-molecule manipulation, cell mechanobiology, and mutation-sensitive detection. Current work connects probe design with live-cell force readout, amplification-free nucleic-acid detection, genome-editing specificity, and clinical sample analysis.

  • Complex DNA, RNA, and RNA-DNA hybrid constructs for single-molecule assays.
  • Tandem hairpin probes for mutation-sensitive molecular detection.
  • DNA mechanical fluorescence probes for force magnitude, duration, and loading rate.

Epigenetic DNA mechanics and genome stability

Click to view how methylation changes DNA structure, elasticity, condensation, and repair.

The lab studies how DNA modifications regulate physical properties and biological function. Work on 5-methylcytosine and N6-methyladenine links chemical modification with DNA condensation, duplex stability, hybridization, RecA assembly, homologous pairing, D-loop extension, strand exchange, and genome stability.

  • DNA methylation and condensation.
  • Modification-dependent DNA elasticity and hybridization.
  • Epigenetic regulation of homologous recombination.

Dynamic regulation of DNA, RNA, and RNA-DNA hybrids

Click to view how force, ions, temperature, solvent, and proteins reshape nucleic-acid helices.

DNA, RNA, and RNA-DNA hybrid duplexes share helical architecture but respond differently to the environment. The lab investigates how salt, temperature, stretching force, protein binding, solvent polarity, and multivalent ions regulate diameter, groove geometry, twist density, stiffness, charge inversion, and twist-stretch coupling.

  • DNA and RNA elasticity under force and torque.
  • RNA-DNA hybrid mechanics and bendability.
  • Ion-mediated stability, charge screening, and charge inversion.

Single-molecule polysaccharide biology

Click to view the new single-chain mechanics platform for polysaccharides and soft biomaterials.

The project application outlines a new direction in single-molecule polysaccharide biology. The lab is developing end-functionalization, single-chain stretching, fluorescence detection, and multiscale simulations to connect sugar-chain composition and ion response with hydrogel mechanics, tissue engineering, wound repair, drug delivery, food colloids, and biomimetic extracellular matrices.

  • Single-chain elasticity and ion response of representative polysaccharides.
  • Effects of glycosidic linkage, substituents, charge density, and chain length.
  • Quantitative links between single-chain mechanics and hydrogel performance.

Single-molecule biology of RNA modifications

Click to view how RNA chemical modifications may reshape folding landscapes and function.

The lab plans to integrate modified RNA synthesis, single-molecule force measurements, fluorescence dynamics, molecular simulations, and functional validation to determine how RNA modifications regulate elasticity, stability, folding barriers, annealing kinetics, conformational selection, immune recognition, and RNA therapeutics.

  • m6A, m5C, pseudouridine, m1-pseudouridine, m1A, m7G, 2'-O-methylation, and 8-oxoG.
  • RNA hairpins, G-quadruplexes, riboswitches, viral RNA elements, and RNA-protein complexes.
  • Comparisons among RNA, DNA, and RNA-DNA hybrids.

Cell single-molecule mechanobiology

Click to view how DNA force probes can quantify molecular forces in living cells and disease microenvironments.

The lab uses DNA mechanical probes, programmable hydrogel matrices, live-cell single-molecule imaging, and quantitative physical models to study how disease-related cells generate, sense, and remember forces. This direction connects molecular force parameters with signaling, fate decisions, tumor invasion, fibrosis, immune recognition, vascular biology, stem cells, organoids, and disease-model chips.

  • Integrins, cadherins, T cell receptors, immune checkpoint receptors, and extracellular matrix proteins.
  • Force magnitude, duration, loading rate, and mechanical memory.
  • Quantitative metrics for disease mechanisms and biomaterial design.

Methods

Integrated experimental and computational toolkit

Single-molecule magnetic tweezers

Force and torque spectroscopy for probing molecular mechanics, topology, twist-stretch coupling, elasticity, and molecular stability.

Single-molecule fluorescence

Observation of molecular states, dynamics, force probes, cellular mechanics, and single-molecule interactions in living and reconstituted systems.

Biochemical assays

Functional measurements that complement and validate single-molecule observations in DNA repair, genotyping, and nucleic-acid regulation.

Molecular dynamics simulations

Computational models that connect experimental observables with molecular mechanisms of DNA, RNA, RNA-DNA hybrids, ions, and proteins.

Research Progress

Representative directions from current lab work

DNA probes for biological research and clinical sample detection

The lab designs DNA probes for cell biology and medical sample detection. Earlier work established methods for preparing complex DNA, RNA, and RNA-DNA hybrid constructs for single-molecule manipulation. These tools supported studies of RPA assembly on single-stranded DNA, tandem hairpin probes for amplification-free RNA mutation detection in serum and saliva, single-base-mutation probes for genome-editing specificity, glass-microneedle-based DNA probing, and reversible DNA mechanical fluorescence probes that report force magnitude, duration, and loading rate in living cells.

Methylation, DNA mechanics, and genome stability

The lab investigates how DNA methylation changes elasticity, structure, dynamics, and function. Studies of 5-methylcytosine have connected methylation with DNA condensation, duplex stability, and inhibited hybridization. Work on N6-methyladenine has shown how DNA modification can regulate homologous recombination through changes in charge, stretching response, RecA assembly, homologous pairing, D-loop extension, and strand exchange.

Dynamic regulation of nucleic-acid structure

DNA and RNA duplexes share helical features but respond differently to their physical environment. The lab studies how salt concentration, temperature, stretching force, protein binding, solvent polarity, and multivalent ions regulate duplex diameter, major-groove geometry, twist density, stiffness, charge inversion, and twist-stretch coupling. These studies help explain why DNA, RNA, and RNA-DNA hybrids show distinct structural dynamics and biological roles.

Future Directions

Expanding single-molecule biology

Single-molecule polysaccharide biology

The lab is extending single-molecule manipulation to polysaccharides, including directed end-functionalization, single-chain stretching, fluorescence detection, and multiscale simulations. This direction aims to connect chain composition, glycosidic linkage, substituents, charge density, chain length, ions, pH, osmotic pressure, macromolecular crowding, and protein binding with elasticity, condensation, crosslinking, gelation, hydrogel mechanics, tissue-engineering materials, wound repair materials, drug delivery, and biomimetic extracellular matrices.

Single-molecule biology of RNA modifications

The lab plans to integrate modified RNA synthesis, force measurements, fluorescence dynamics, molecular simulations, and functional validation to determine how m6A, m5C, pseudouridine, m1-pseudouridine, m1A, m7G, 2'-O-methylation, and oxidative damage such as 8-oxoG regulate RNA elasticity, folding landscapes, conformational dynamics, molecular recognition, immune response, and RNA therapeutics.

Cell single-molecule mechanobiology

DNA mechanical probes, programmable hydrogel matrices, live-cell single-molecule imaging, and quantitative physical models will be used to study how disease-related cells generate, sense, and remember mechanical forces. Targets include integrins, cadherins, T cell receptors, immune checkpoint receptors, extracellular matrix proteins, tumor invasion, fibrosis, immune recognition, endothelial mechanics, stem-cell fate, organoids, and disease-model chips.
